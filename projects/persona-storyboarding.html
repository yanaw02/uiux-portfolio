Bubble Tea
Ordering Kiosk

Understanding the Persona

One of the most important aspects of creating an interface that exists in a public space is to consider what kind of persona would use this interface.

For my UIUX course assignment, I take a look at a Kiosk at one of the local bubble tea shops in my area and try to determine the user profile of someone who this product is designed for through a series of interviews, observations, empathy maps, and a storyboard.

Understanding the Product

The kiosk consists of:

  • a touchscreen application from which you can make orders with
  • a card reader allowing for payment
  • a menu page detailing the availability, cost, and photographs of each drink for the user to scroll through.
  • a drink page allows the user to heavily customize their orders based on things such as ice level, sugar level, quantity, etc, detailing the cost and contents of these customizations, as well as allowing for the user to input special requests.
  • a payment page where the user can choose their tip
  • a phone number page requires a number to process payment
  • users can also link their Snackpass and receive rewards

It specifically exists as an alternative method for tea-lovers to make purchases at the shop, making the ordering process more streamlined, efficient, and customizable. It also allows for employees to focus on creating the drinks rather than tending to orders.

When their order is ready, the application automatically messages the number associated with the order for the customer to pick up, allowing for increased customer mobility and order accuracy as well.

Researching the Persona

To properly understand the type of users this kiosk was designed for, I conducted a few short interviews on some colleagues and friends upon asking them to order something at the kiosk.

Given the fact that the store is located on a college campus, and that most of the people I could find here were college students, there is slightly less variation and diversity in my data collection than I would have liked. However, given the circumstances, here are some of the key observations I found of my users.

Key Observations

  • All users scrolled around to explored items and customizations for their drinks. Few ended up spending quite some time to consider what to order while some others clicked though and were very fast with their familiar orders.
  • Users actively chose to customize their orders.
  • No one walked away when it was required the purchase be paid with card.
  • Some hesitated at the reqiurement to put down their phone number.
  • Few left a small tip.

Outside of these passively perceived observations, all 3 users also responded to my interview questions. The stated questions and summarized responses are here below.

Interview Summary

  • How do you feel using the kiosk?
    All interviewees responded that the interface was very bright and easy to navigate and understand. If anything, the users who were new to using the kiosk reported it took a bit for them to read through everything, but otherwise throught the device was efficient.
  • On average, how often would you prefer using the kiosk over ordering at the counter?
    Certain interviewees prefered using the Kiosk over ordering at the counter as it gave them more control over their orders. Others preferred it because they preferred not to talk to people.
  • What was on your mind when you were using the kiosk?
    Everyone was just concerned with picking their order.
  • What's something you would change about the kiosk?
    Many complained about the touchscreen having blips. One particular user did not like that he was required to enter his phone number, as he was afraid of getting promotional spam.

Creating the Persona

With the information I have gathered in mind, I created two personas for the users of this kiosk, one named Beth, and another named John.

I have also made a storyboard for Beth, detailing her supposed experience using the kiosk, at the very end of the page.

BETH

Description
Beth is a hardworking young adult who just ended a long day of work meetings, and just wants to get a drink or a snack before she goes home to recharge her social battery.
Problems Encountered
The touchscreen is not always responsive. Beth struggles with punching in the buttons and feels pressured by the growing line of people behind her. She struggles between asking for an employee's help and her social anxiety.
Why is Beth a representative persona?
Beth is clearly a younger, more shy, more tech-savvy young adult who prefers the comfort, freedom, and privacy of using the kiosk over ordering at the counter. At the kiosk, she's under no pressure to answer questions for her order immediately and can do some exploration with shop items.

JOHN

Description
John is a college-aged student who has classes to attend in 15 minutes and he knows exactly what drink he usually needs to get him through his day.
Problems Encountered
John is in a rush. His order is specific and long, and the application sometimes can glitch out or fail to sense his inputs, causing him to lose precious time as if he accidentally clicks through it, he has to go back and restart his mistake. He's frustrated that he has to rush and can't just say his order, which would be faster.
Why is John a representative persona?
Like Beth, John is also a younger adult who has experience with online shopping and is very comfortable with using a digital interface and getting drinks to go on the road. His long and specific order can sometimes confuse staff and cause them to make mistakes. However, with a digital interface which records specific choices, he has more control over what he wants through the kiosk.
